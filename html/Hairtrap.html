Hairtrap Brush

A solution for hair removal from hands, shower walls and floors.

For girls, by girls.

s

Problem

How might we make the clean up process during and after showering easier for users whose hair sheds during the shampooing/conditioner process?

Duration

Jan 2019 to Apr 2019

Role

Product Designer

MY ROLE AND GENERAL BRIEF

My team, comprising of 5 girls, was passionate to design a product that can be used to remove hair off our hands and off the shower walls with ease, making shower cleaning easier. I led the brainstorming session and helped narrow down ideas using alternative matrices. I was also leading the prototyping of the second iteration, focusing on the retracting mechanism of the brush bristles. After each prototyping round, I helped synthesise the results and suggest new improvements for the design.

FINAL DESIGN: HAIR TRAP

PROCESS

EMPATHISING: UNDERSTANDING THE PROBLEM

In order to gain a deeper understanding of the problem, I went out to observe, survey and interview people. We did patent research and with the help of alternative matrices, we managed to narrow down our problem and gain a deeper insight into the issues faced by people.

DEFINING THE PROBLEM

For girls, hair often get stuck on our hands when we shampoo and condition. Many will choose to:
1. Peel them off and stick them on the walls or
2. Wash them down with water
The hair will eventually clog up drains, which are gross to clean.

IDEATE & PROTOTYPE: HAIR REMOVAL METHODS

When brainstorming, we came up with a range of ways ways the users can remove hair in their showers, methods including using a brush, a glove, sticky material, slow release substance to dissolve hair. I was heading the design of the retractable mechanism of the brush.

ITERATION: FOCUS ON BRUSH HAIR DISPOSAL AND BRISTLE TYPE TESTING

After prototyping, many users were concerned with the removal of hair collected on the brush or surface. Many users find direct contact with hair “gross”. Hence, during the second iteration, we focused on methods to remove hair without touching it.

Similarly, we placed 10 strands of wet hair on the wall and hands of the user. As the bristles were highly effective, we timed the amount of time users take to remove hair instead.

FINAL PRODUCT: HAIRTRAP

Based on quantitative results, we decided that the cylindrical, closely spaced, hard bristles were better for brushing hair off walls, and the cone shaped, closely spaced, soft bristles were better for removing hair off our hands. Hence, we decided to incorporate both into our product.

I also designed a retracting mechanism with a spring that allows users to push the hair off the brush, into the bin.
