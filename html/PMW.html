Man O’ War (PMW) Tank

A tank designed to hold a poisonous and fragile Portuguese Man O’ War (PMW)

Goal

To design an aquarium tank that most closely replicates the PMW’s natural habitat allow researchers to study its one full life-cycle<

Duration

Jan 2019 to Jun 2019

Role

Design Engineer

MY ROLE AND RESPONSIBILITIES

To date, no PMW has yet to be successfully kept in captivity due to its fragility, and there are limited online and offline research. I was the project manager and made sure my team was on time and on target. I led the brainstorming sessions and also did the sketches for the ideas. I learnt a lot about effective work distribution utilising the strengths of each individual. When building and testing prototypes, I also learnt how to test quantitatively to hit specifications. This project gave me a sneak peak of what working as a design engineer in a real company feels like, and I am grateful for this opportunity.

PRELIMINARY RESEARCH: LEARNING ABOUT THE PMW

As there were little primary research, we searched the internet for experts on invertebrates. The PMW is a jellyfish-like creature that floats on the surface of the ocean. It is not mobile on its own and moves according to the waves and the wind. It is so fragile that it may bruise and die when it comes into contact with other surfaces. After talking to a few experts, we managed to conclude that there are 2 main hypothesised design requirements that determines the PMW's survival.

DESIGN REQUIREMENTS: WIND AND KEEPING OFF TANK WALLS

It must allow the researcher to vary wind speed to replicate oceanic winds. This is because wind is hypothesised to trigger the "dipping behaviour" of the PMW, which is when the PMW lies its sail flat on the side to moisten its sail.

It must help the PMW avoid the tank walls. When the PMW hits any surfaces, it may bruise and die.

BRAINSTORMING

The main 2 takeaways of our initial ideation were:
1. Using water circulation or
2. Using wind circulation to keep PMW off the sides of the tank

KREISEL SYSTEM

Spray bars with slits cut in them are placed in the tub, creating a curtain of water circulating around the side of the tank.

AIR CIRCULATION DOME

Using a dome, we create a uniform curtain of wind coming down the sides of the tank, pushing the PMW to the center. The cylinder shaped structures act as flow straighteners.

PROTOTYPE 2: FOCUSING ON THE KREISEL SYSTEM AND SCALING IT UP

We decided to go ahead with the Kreisel System as it is able to
1. Theoretically create a dead zone in the middle of the pool
2. Provide easy access to the PMW if necessary
3. Allow easy wind simulation to study the effect of wind on the PMW
Our next step was to build a large scale prototype to understand the kreisel effect on the actual scale.

PROTOTYPE 2: WIND SIMULATION STAND

The stand was necessary as we wanted to enable researchers to study the effect of different wind speed, patterns and directions on PMW. Studies have shown that the PMW reacts positively to wind.

Some challenges we face includes how might we reduce the trip hazard of the stand and also how to ensure the wind simulation is as similar to oceanic winds as possible. We tried different ways to create an airway, cutting slits and holes of different patterns into PVC pipes. We tested the wind speed using a manometer to ensure that we are able to hit oceanic wind speeds of 2 to 10km/hr.

FINAL DESIGN

THE MAIN PMW TANK

The tank was provided by Shedd. Our team decided to make full use of Shedd's resources to reduce cost for Shedd's side and to make a simpler solution that can be easily integrated into their existing water system.

SPRAY BAR GUARD

The spray bar guards were created to ensure a smooth uniform flow at the sides of the tank. The water speed is highest at the sides and decays as it goes inwards. We realised that occasionally, the mock PMW may get stuck at the highest flow at the sides. Hence, in order to direct the flow of high speed water at the sides towards the center, we created the guards, which can be tilted at varying angles in order to change the flow pattern within the tub.

WIND SIMULATION STAND

The wind stand allows the researcher to simulate oceanic winds and observe the PMW's response to wind changes. Wind is hypothesised to trigger "dipping behaviour". The stand is designed to be minimally obstrusive to the researcher, thus ensuring workplace safety. From our prototyping, small holes in a row provided the most uniform gust of wind that is similar to oceanic breeze. The wind pipe can also be tilted to change the wind angle.

HOW IT WORKS & TEST RESULTS

Water flows out from a thin slit on the spray bar, creating a laminar flow which decays towards the edge of the tank. This centers the PMW and makes sure it does not touch the sides of the tank. The wind stand is then turned on whenever necessary to study the effects of wind on the PMW. Future steps may include performing fluid dynamics analysis on the whole model to ensure accuracy.
